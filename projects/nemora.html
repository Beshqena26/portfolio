Context: designing where every pixel answers to revenue

Nemora is an AI companion platform — users create a character, chat, and generate images. It's a competitive, conversion-driven market: traffic is bought with ads, and the product has seconds to turn a visitor into a registered user and days to turn them into a subscriber. My work covered the full loop: product UI, the paywall system, pricing, and the entire growth layer — ad creatives, SEO landing pages, blog templates, seasonal campaigns.

It's also an 18+ product, which raises the design bar in a specific way: users want discretion and trust as much as delight. That shaped decisions from billing copy to how imagery is teased.

The pricing page: a conversion system, not a table

This screen carries the business. Every element is a deliberate conversion mechanic:

Anchoring & urgency

Three tiers priced to make 12 months the obvious deal ($13.99 → $5.99 with the crossed-out $25.99 anchor), a first-visit discount countdown, and "only today" framing. Aggressive — but every claim is real and cancelable.

Trust for a discreet product

The line that converts here isn't the price — it's "No adult transaction in your bank statement." Plus Stripe-badged checkout, card logos, cancel-anytime, and "100% anonymous" up top. Discretion is the feature.

The paywall that doesn't feel like a wall

Premium content in chat isn't hidden — it's teased. Images arrive blurred with an inline "Upgrade to Premium" chip, so free users experience the full conversation rhythm and see exactly what upgrading unlocks. The benefit list on pricing ("Remove image blur") then matches something they've already wanted. Suggestion chips keep conversations flowing — engagement is the retention engine, and message volume is what makes the subscription feel worth it.

Character creator: complexity behind presets

The creator is the product's emotional hook — and a form with a dozen dimensions. The design keeps it feeling like play, not configuration: visual preset grids for the big identity choices, compact dropdowns for the rest, one sticky "Create" action at the end.

Discover: merchandising a content catalog

The home screen works like an e-commerce storefront: a rotating hero campaign, a character grid with personality tags and hover-to-chat, an in-grid "Create your own" card that turns browsing into creating, and an SEO-oriented FAQ block — because in this market, organic search is a major acquisition channel.

The design system behind it

Shipped products need boring rigor: documented nav states (signed-out / signed-in / menu-open), button hierarchies, settings IA, card anatomy, and opacity ramps for every brand color — so a fast-moving team ships consistent UI without me in the room.

Beyond the product: the growth layer

Half of this job was never inside the app:

  • 100+ ad creatives across formats (300×315 to 1080×1080), iterated in versioned batches against real campaign performance.
  • 9 hero-section variants designed and tested for the landing — including persona-targeted versions (male / female / anime aesthetics, left / center / right compositions).
  • SEO landing system + blog templates — templated pages the team could replicate per keyword without a designer.
  • Seasonal campaigns — Valentine's Day and Easter takeovers, plus an achievements system for retention.

That's the part I'd underline in this case study: it's designing a business, continuously, not delivering a file.

What I'd do differently

The tension in this product is real: conversion patterns like countdowns and anchors work, but overusing them erodes the trust the product depends on. If I started again I'd define the "pressure budget" up front — which mechanics we use, where, and what we never do — instead of negotiating it screen by screen. And I'd bring cohort data into design reviews earlier: we designed against campaign metrics long before we saw true retention curves.
